Role Guide

Use this when you're scoping or staffing a site redesign. Find the scenario that best matches what your client is experiencing — most projects will touch more than one.

🌿

The Overgrown Site

The client has been adding content for years without a plan. They know it's a mess — they just don't know how to stop digging.
▼
Client is saying
"Our website is confusing and overly complicated — users can't find what they need."
Also sounds like
"We've added so much over the years and it's gotten away from us."

Who to bring in & what they'll do

✍️ Content Design
  • Content audit against goals, user needs, and current performance
  • Content ecosystem map to visualize what exists and how it connects
  • Taxonomy and labeling systems
  • Sitemaps and navigation redesign
  • Governance model: roles, responsibilities, and publishing rules
🗺️ UX
  • Audience research: top tasks, priorities, and goals
  • Define success metrics for navigation and findability
  • User flows grounded in actual audience behavior
🔬 Research
  • Tree testing and card sorting to validate restructured navigation
  • Analytics review: audience pathways and drop-off points
  • Findability benchmarking

Discovery Roadmap

Phase 1: Content audit — Content Design leads
Inventory everything against user needs and business goals. Separate what's working from what's creating noise. This produces the prioritized content inventory that everything else is built on.
Phase 2: Ecosystem mapping + taxonomy — Content Design + UX
Build the content ecosystem map and new taxonomy system. UX defines the audience and sets success metrics in parallel so structure decisions are grounded in who we're actually designing for.
Phase 3: Structural validation — Research
Tree testing and card sorting confirm the new navigation works before anything gets designed. Findings feed directly into the IA and sitemap.
Where this leads: A validated IA framework, sitemap, and governance model that become the brief for wireframing and visual design. The client also leaves with a content ownership structure that prevents the same problem from recurring.

The Ghost Ship

The client has lost track of what their site even contains. Staff turnover, old vendors, or years of neglect mean nobody has a full picture — including them.
▼
Client is saying
"Honestly, we don't even know what's on our site anymore."
Also sounds like
"You'll need to talk to the previous agency to get context." / "I think someone owns that section but I'm not sure who."

Who to bring in & what they'll do

🗺️ UX
  • Site inventory and audit: what exists, what's used, what's orphaned
  • Stakeholder interviews to map content ownership
  • Scope definition: what can be touched and what can't
🔬 Research
  • Analytics review: what content is actually getting traffic and use
  • Audience pathways to identify what matters vs. what can be cut
✍️ Content Design
  • Content ecosystem map once inventory is complete
  • IA and taxonomy recommendations based on what the audit surfaces
  • Roles and responsibilities framework going forward

Discovery Roadmap

Phase 1: Site inventory + ownership mapping — UX leads
Audit what exists and run stakeholder interviews to establish who owns what. Nothing can be scoped until we know what we're working with and who has the authority to change it.
Phase 2: Analytics review — Research
Overlay traffic data to separate what's actually valuable from what's just old. Provides the evidence base for what to keep, cut, or consolidate.
Phase 3: Ecosystem map + IA — Content Design
With a complete picture established, Content Design maps the ecosystem and makes structural recommendations. This is the transition point from discovery to direction.
Where this leads: A scoped, realistic project brief the team and client can align on — with a clear picture of what the redesign needs to solve, what's in and out of scope, and who owns what going forward.

The Conversion Chaser

High exit rates, incomplete journeys, low engagement. Users are leaving and the client wants to know why — and fix it.
▼
Client is saying
"Users are abandoning flows or dropping off mid-task, and we don't know why."
Also sounds like
"We have a general idea of who our audiences are, but we don't know what their needs are or what they come to the site to do."

Who to bring in & what they'll do

🔬 Research
  • Customer behavioral research
  • UX benchmarking against current experience
  • Surveys and usability testing
  • Analytics: audience pathways and funnel analysis
🗺️ UX
  • User journey mapping grounded in discovery data
  • Roadmapping and scope clarification
  • UX audit (behavioral heuristics, heatmaps, a11y)
✍️ Content Design
  • Identify content touch points across the user journey
  • Language standardization and comprehension testing
  • Microcopy and CTA review

Discovery Roadmap

Phase 1: Behavioral research — Research leads
Customer behavioral research, funnel analysis, and analytics review identify exactly where and why journeys break down. Output is a prioritized map of problem areas grounded in evidence — not assumptions.
Phase 2: Journey mapping — UX
Using research findings, UX maps the corrected user journey and identifies where the experience — and the content within it — is creating friction or failure points.
Phase 3: Content touch point audit — Content Design
Audit the language, CTAs, and content touch points along the journey for comprehension issues, inconsistencies, and missed opportunities.
Where this leads: A prioritized intervention list ordered by impact — the direct input for the design and content sprint. Prevents the team from jumping to solutions before the actual problem is understood.

The Divided Room

Multiple stakeholders, competing visions. Internal alignment is broken and the team can't agree on direction.
▼
Client is saying
"We have competing stakeholder visions and need to find a way forward."

Who to bring in & what they'll do

🔬 Research
  • Stakeholder workshops to surface and align competing priorities
  • 1:1 stakeholder interviews to understand individual perspectives
  • UX site audit to ground opinions in observable behavior
✍️ Content Design
  • Messaging workshop to align on core narrative
  • Messaging architecture that accommodates multiple stakeholder goals
🗺️ UX
  • Full site audit (behavioral heuristics, analytics, a11y)
  • Lead/Manager alignment sessions with key stakeholders

Discovery Roadmap

Phase 1: Stakeholder research — Research leads
1:1 interviews and workshops surface the real tension points — what each stakeholder actually wants, what they're afraid of, and where there's more alignment than the room suggests.
Phase 2: Messaging alignment — Content Design
A messaging workshop uses research findings to build a core narrative that stakeholders can commit to. Without this, any design will be challenged downstream.
Phase 3: Site audit — UX
An objective audit of the current site moves the conversation from opinions to evidence — giving everyone a shared baseline to make decisions from.
Where this leads: A documented point of view on the site's purpose, audience, and messaging hierarchy that serves as the brief for IA and design. Stakeholder sign-off at this stage protects the rest of the project.

The Content Crisis

Content is unmanageable, the CMS is a mess, governance has broken down, and nobody knows where to start.
▼
Client is saying
"We've put this redesign off because we don't know where to start — it's a huge mess."
Also sounds like
"We don't let anyone use the CMS because they might mess everything up."

Who to bring in & what they'll do

✍️ Content Design
  • Content ecosystem map
  • Content audit against goals, objectives, and user needs
  • Roles and responsibilities recommendations
  • IA and content templates
  • Content migration plan
🗺️ UX
  • Content curation strategy during wireframing stage
  • Admin role and workflow recommendations

Discovery Roadmap

Phase 1: Content audit — Content Design leads
Audit all existing content against goals, user needs, and current performance. This is the hardest phase to sell but the most important — the client needs to understand we can't redesign our way out of a content problem.
Phase 2: Ecosystem mapping + governance — Content Design + UX
Map the full content ecosystem and establish a governance model: who owns what, who can publish, and what the rules are. UX supports with CMS workflow and admin role recommendations.
Phase 3: IA and templates — Content Design + UX
Build the IA framework and content templates that make the redesign executable — and keep the CMS manageable after launch.
Where this leads: A content-ready brief for the build phase, including a migration plan and governance structure the client can actually use. This phase is what makes the redesign stick long-term.

The Multi-Audience Juggler

The site needs to serve very different user groups at the same time with different needs, goals, and entry points.
▼
Client is saying
"Our site needs to talk to very different personas at the same time."

Who to bring in & what they'll do

✍️ Content Design
  • Messaging architecture for each audience
  • Content playbook
  • Template design with audience-aware structure
  • IA and navigation strategy
  • Prioritization support across competing content needs
🗺️ UX
  • Page layout strategy: getting the right info to the right users
  • User flow design for multiple entry points
🔬 Research
  • Understand what different users do on the site and why they visit
  • Audience segmentation and needs mapping

Discovery Roadmap

Phase 1: Audience research — Research leads
Define what each distinct audience actually comes to the site to do — not who the client thinks they are, but how they behave. Output is an evidence-based picture of real goals and entry points per audience segment.
Phase 2: Messaging architecture — Content Design
Build a messaging framework that allows each audience to navigate confidently without the experience feeling fragmented. Defines content hierarchy, IA, and tone by audience segment.
Phase 3: Page layout strategy — UX
Design page layouts and flow patterns that surface the right content to the right people at the right moment — grounded in the messaging architecture.
Where this leads: An audience-by-audience content and IA framework that directly informs wireframes. Prevents the build phase from reverting to "let's just put everything on the homepage."

The Data-less Guesser

No user data. Decisions are based on internal opinions. The client wants to move fast but has no foundation to build on.
▼
Client is saying
"How can we — or should we — proceed without data?"
Also sounds like
"We have a general idea of who our audiences are, but we don't really know what they need."

Who to bring in & what they'll do

🔬 Research
  • User interviews to establish a baseline understanding
  • UX benchmark of the current experience
  • Analytics review to layer quantitative context
  • Surveys to gather attitudinal data at scale
🗺️ UX
  • Roadmapping: define what we know vs. what we need to learn before scoping
  • Scope clarification and phased discovery planning

Discovery Roadmap

Phase 1: Foundational research — Research leads
User interviews, analytics review, and a UX benchmark establish a baseline: who users are, how they behave, and where the current experience fails them. This is the evidence the rest of the project builds on.
Phase 2: Scope definition — UX
Using research findings, UX defines what we now know vs. what we still need to learn — and phases the work accordingly. Prevents over-scoping against assumptions that haven't been tested.
Where this leads: A research-grounded project brief with a phased roadmap. The client leaves discovery with a defensible point of view on what to build — and the evidence to back it up internally.

The Identity Shifter

The company has rebranded, changed its name, been acquired, or significantly shifted its market positioning.
▼
Client is saying
"We had a rebrand / brand name change / we got bought — and the site needs to catch up."
Also sounds like
"What is the purpose of a website anymore these days?"

Who to bring in & what they'll do

🗺️ UX
  • Brand strategy sprint: align on new values and positioning
  • Market audit sprint
  • Scope the full system across multiple domains if needed
✍️ Content Design
  • Brand messaging audit across all site surfaces
  • IA review for new positioning needs
  • Messaging architecture aligned to new brand
🔬 Research
  • User interviews across current site to identify existing brand perception
  • Competitive analysis in new market context

Discovery Roadmap

Phase 1: Brand + competitive research — Research leads
User interviews establish current brand perception, and competitive analysis shows how the new positioning lands in the market. Surfaces gaps between how the client sees themselves and how users actually experience them.
Phase 2: Brand strategy sprint — UX leads
Align the team and client on new brand values, positioning, and what that means for the site's purpose and structure. This must happen before any design begins — visual decisions without strategic grounding create a site that looks new but says nothing different.
Phase 3: Messaging architecture — Content Design
Audit all existing content and build a messaging framework aligned to the new brand. Defines tone, content hierarchy, and IA implications of the repositioning across every site surface.
Where this leads: A brand-aligned content and IA framework that becomes the brief for the redesign. Ensures visual and interaction design phases are building on a stable strategic foundation — not reacting to one.

Use this when you're staffing or scoping work on an ongoing retainer engagement. These scenarios often show up after launch or during long-running partnerships.

The Direction Seeker

Budget exists but there's no clear focus. The backlog is full of tickets that aren't tied to any larger goals. The project feels like it's stalling.
▼
Client is saying
"We have X amount of budget. But what should we focus on?"
Also sounds like
"We need improvement ideas to execute to fill in our available unused budget."

Who to bring in & what they'll do

🗺️ UX
  • Internal strategy session before any client-facing workshop is proposed
  • Use that session to diagnose what kind of workshop the client actually needs — so we're not running a prioritization session when they need a visioning session, or vice versa
  • Group backlog tickets into themes of impact and user goals
  • Roadmap planning and scope setting
✍️ Content Design
  • Strategy setting support
  • IA audit to identify what's working and what isn't
  • Taxonomy workshops to establish shared priorities
🔬 Research
  • Analytics review to identify high-impact areas
  • Lightweight user research to validate prioritization

Discovery Roadmap

Phase 1: Internal alignment session — UX leads
Before anything goes to the client, the team aligns on what the data says matters most — and diagnoses what kind of engagement the client actually needs. A client who asks for a "prioritization session" may really need a visioning session first. Getting this wrong means running the right process for the wrong problem.
Phase 2: Data review — Research
Analytics review and lightweight user research provide the evidence base for the prioritization conversation. Separates high-impact opportunities from low-effort noise.
Phase 3: Client prioritization session — UX + Content Design + Research
Facilitate a structured conversation that connects the backlog to user goals and business outcomes — not just technical tickets. The team arrives with a point of view, not a blank facilitation agenda.
Where this leads: A prioritized roadmap with clear rationale the client can take to their leadership. Replaces a vague retainer with a focused workstream the team can execute against with confidence.

The Post-Launch Validator

The site just launched. Now the client wants to know: did it work? What do users think? What still needs to be fixed?
▼
Client is saying
"We launched the site with you — now what?"
Also sounds like
"We want to prove our redesign hypothesis and highlight areas that still need refinement. Was the project successful?"

Who to bring in & what they'll do

🔬 Research
  • NPS surveys and Voice of Customer programs
  • Usability testing against the launched experience
  • Analytics tracking and performance reporting
  • Benchmark comparison: before vs. after launch
  • Identify conversion, retention, or engagement gaps where Growth can drive impact
✍️ Content Design
  • Content comprehension and IA testing
  • Set up and run content-specific tests
  • Lines of inquiry for ongoing measurement
🗺️ UX
  • Support analytics setup and tracking configuration
  • Synthesize findings into actionable improvement recommendations
  • Flag opportunities where Growth team involvement would accelerate results (e.g. A/B testing, funnel optimization, SEO)

Discovery Roadmap

Phase 1: Baseline measurement — Research leads
NPS surveys, usability testing on the live site, and analytics review establish the post-launch baseline. Benchmark comparison against pre-launch data shows what improved, what didn't, and what created new problems.
Phase 2: Content + IA testing — Content Design
Targeted tests evaluate specific content and structural decisions made during the build. Sets lines of inquiry for ongoing measurement beyond this phase.
Phase 3: Growth opportunity assessment — UX + Research
Synthesize findings to identify where Growth team involvement — A/B testing, funnel optimization, SEO — would accelerate results the design work alone can't move.
Where this leads: A clear post-launch report — what's working, what needs refinement, and a prioritized next-steps roadmap. Gives the client validation of the investment and a grounded plan for what the retainer should focus on next.

The Feature Expander

A new product, offering, or feature needs to be integrated into an existing site — and it needs to feel like it belongs.
▼
Client is saying
"We want to add a new product offering, and it needs to feel prominent and special."
Also sounds like
"We have a new product that needs adding to a nav / site."

Who to bring in & what they'll do

🔬 Research
  • Discovery interviews and product-market fit research
  • Competitive analysis in the new feature space
  • User interviews to understand how the new offering fits into existing behavior
✍️ Content Design
  • IA audit: where does this fit in the existing structure?
  • Taxonomy workshops to integrate new content
  • Messaging and positioning for the new offering
🗺️ UX
  • User flow design for the new feature path
  • Integration with existing navigation and journeys
🎨 Product Design
  • Component and pattern design for the new feature
  • Visual integration with the existing design system

Discovery Roadmap

Phase 1: Discovery research — Research leads
Discovery interviews, product-market fit research, and competitive analysis answer the foundational question: does this new offering match what users actually need, and where does it fit in their existing mental model of the site?
Phase 2: IA audit + messaging — Content Design
Audit the current site structure to find the right home for the new content. Build the messaging framework and positioning for the new offering so it feels like it belongs — not like it arrived late.
Phase 3: Flow + component design — UX + Product Design
Design the user flow and component patterns that integrate the new feature into existing journeys. The goal is native, not bolted on.
Where this leads: A feature-ready design and content brief with validated positioning. Ensures the new offering strengthens the overall site rather than creating a seam the client will need to fix in the next retainer cycle.

The Strategy Steward

Long-running relationship. The client wants to maintain momentum, revisit the roadmap, or align a new stakeholder on what's been decided.
▼
Client is saying
"We want to regroup on long-term strategy and keep momentum going."
Also sounds like
"The new marketing manager wants a new workstream on what they think is important." / "Things we couldn't get to in the initial redesign are piling up."

Who to bring in & what they'll do

🗺️ UX
  • Lead an internal strategy session before the client roadmapping session
  • Backlog review and grooming with the team
  • Parking lot / prioritization framework to capture what got cut
✍️ Content Design
  • Strategy setting support during client sessions
  • Content roadmap input: what's deferred, what's urgent
🔬 Research
  • Lightweight check-in research to validate roadmap assumptions
  • Data synthesis to support priority conversations

Discovery Roadmap

Phase 1: Internal team alignment — UX leads
Before meeting with the client, the team reviews what was built, what got deferred, and what the data is telling us. Produces a point of view on what the roadmap should prioritize — so we arrive at the client session with a perspective, not a blank agenda.
Phase 2: Data synthesis — Research
Lightweight research or analytics review validates roadmap assumptions and surfaces any user signals that should shift priorities before the client conversation happens.
Phase 3: Client roadmapping session — UX + Content Design
Facilitate a structured session that reconnects the client to the longer-term strategy — or resets it if priorities have shifted. New stakeholders get aligned; deferred work gets triaged against current goals.
Where this leads: A refreshed, prioritized roadmap that the team and client both own. Transforms a drifting retainer into a focused engagement with clear next steps and renewed momentum.

Not sure what a role actually does — or when to bring them in vs. someone else? Use this as a quick reference.

🗺️ UX
Shapes the overall experience: how it works, how users move through it, and how it holds together across flows. Bridges user needs, business goals, and technical constraints.
Best entry point when: you need to figure out what to build before you build it — research synthesis, flows, IA strategy, journey mapping, or when something feels broken but the cause is unclear.
🔬 Research
Grounds decisions in evidence. Runs interviews, usability tests, surveys, and analytics reviews to understand how real users think, behave, and struggle.
Best entry point when: the team is making assumptions about users, when something isn't working and nobody knows why, or when the client needs to be convinced to slow down before building.
✍️ Content Design
Shapes the content: its structure, language, taxonomy, and governance. Works across the front-end (what users read) and the back-end (how content is managed and scaled).
Best entry point when: users can't find things, the CMS is a mess, the messaging doesn't hold together, or there are IA problems that go deeper than navigation.
🎨 Product Design
Designs the visual and interactive layer: UI components, motion, design systems, and the craft that makes a product feel polished, accessible, and emotionally resonant.
Best entry point when: the experience is defined and needs to be made real — component-level design, visual systems, interaction design, or when a feature is ready to be built.
⚠️ Common staffing mistakes
  • Asking a visual / product designer to solve an IA or navigation problem — that's a Content Design and UX problem first.
  • Skipping Research because there's "no time" — this creates risk downstream that costs more to fix.
  • Bringing in Content Design only for copywriting — they work on structure and systems, not just words.
  • Treating UX as wireframe producers — they define scope, strategy, and flows, not just lo-fi screens.
  • Staffing for the deliverable you think you need instead of the problem you're trying to solve.
